# BYD SDV and AI-DV Architecture Deep Dive

## Executive synthesis

BYD is no longer best understood as a battery-and-powertrain company with software attached. ==Since the launch of e-Platform 3.0 in 2021, the company has been steadily moving from a classic distributed ECU model toward an architecture built around integrated vehicle intelligence, centralized or domain-central compute, and vehicle-cloud coupling. The decisive shift came with the public unveiling of the XUANJI architecture in early 2024, which BYD described as the vehicle’s “brain and neural network,” followed by the broad commercialization of the God’s Eye ADAS stack in 2025 and the debut of its self-developed 4 nm automotive driving SoC, XUANJI A3, in May 2026. In parallel, BYD has kept pushing deep vertical integration in batteries, semiconductors, power electronics, thermal systems, infotainment, and automotive electronics through BYD, BYD Electronics, FinDreams Battery, and BYD Semiconductor. ==

Technically, BYD’s architecture is best characterized as **hybrid-centralized rather than fully zonal**. Public evidence is strong for central ADAS compute, increasingly integrated powertrain and body control, and a cockpit stack built around in-house DiLink and cloud services. Public evidence is much weaker for a clean, Tesla-style published zonal backbone or a model-by-model network topology. In other words, BYD has clearly moved beyond “many independent ECUs plus CAN spaghetti,” but it has not publicly documented a homogeneous, single-generation architecture the way software-native OEMs like Tesla try to present themselves. 

The most important strategic fact is not that BYD is the most elegant SDV stack in the market. It is that BYD is making a **good-enough SDV stack massively manufacturable at Chinese cost curves**. In 2024 it spent RMB 54.2 billion on R&D, sold 4.27 million vehicles, and in 2025 it sold more than 4.6 million vehicles with over 1.04 million overseas. It also disclosed a 4,000-plus ADAS engineering team in 2024 and announced more than RMB 100 billion of intelligent-technology investment over the following three years. That scale matters because it allows BYD to amortize ADAS, cockpit, and compute upgrades across mass-market Ocean and Dynasty products instead of reserving them for halo vehicles only.

My bottom-line assessment is this: **BYD is genuinely software-defined in deployment and update economics, partially software-defined in architecture, and not yet as software-native or software-homogeneous as Tesla.** Against Chinese peers, it is unusually strong in cross-domain integration and cost-down industrialization, but still more heterogeneous and partner-dependent than Xiaomi’s software culture or Huawei’s tightly orchestrated ADAS ecosystem.

## E/E evolution and compute topology

BYD’s public architecture story breaks into four phases. Before e-Platform 3.0, BYD largely followed the industry’s normal path: distributed controllers, supplier-heavy subsystems, and feature addition by module. e-Platform 3.0, announced in 2021, was the first public sign of a deeper architectural reset. BYD explicitly said that the platform integrated and standardized core components while creating a new body structure, a new digital and electrical architecture, and a revamped BYD vehicle operating system. That matters because it marks the point where BYD stopped talking only about batteries and motors and started treating software and E/E design as platform primitives.

The second phase arrived with the 2024 Dream Day launch of XUANJI. BYD described XUANJI as a vehicle intelligence architecture that fuses electrification with intelligence, senses internal and external conditions in real time, consolidates information at millisecond level, and feeds it to a central “brain” for rapid decision-making. BYD also attached an AI large model to this architecture and said it covered more than 300 vehicle scenarios. That language is more than marketing. It is BYD’s public declaration that the car should be orchestrated as one cyber-physical system, not as loosely coupled functional islands. 

The third phase was the 2025 commercialization wave around God’s Eye. Here BYD stopped talking architecture in the abstract and started productizing it into repeatable compute tiers: God’s Eye C for mainstream vehicles, God’s Eye B for higher-value products, and God’s Eye A for its most premium lineup. Horizon Robotics confirmed that DiPilot 100 entered mass production on BYD models using the Journey 6 series in early 2025, while NVIDIA said BYD was expanding DRIVE Orin centralized compute into next-generation Dynasty and Ocean vehicles. This shows a clear tiered compute policy: **camera-and-radar ADAS at scale for the mass market, LiDAR and higher compute for upper trims and premium sub-brands, and increasing reuse of centralized compute blocks across lineups.**

The fourth phase began in 2026 with XUANJI A3. BYD announced China’s first 4 nm automotive driving SoC and, at the same event, said its entire lineup could now be optionally equipped with the God’s Eye-B LiDAR version. At the same time, NVIDIA disclosed BYD among the automakers building level-4-ready vehicles on DRIVE Hyperion. Read together, these two moves imply that BYD is now pursuing a dual-track silicon strategy: **internalize the mainstream-to-premium ADAS compute path where it can, while still using NVIDIA’s higher-end reference ecosystem for future L4 programs and validation.** 

What can be said about node topology with confidence is narrower than many analyst slides suggest. Public BYD material supports the existence of a central intelligence layer, broad cross-domain coordination, and cloud-connected cockpit and vehicle services. It also supports strong component integration in propulsion: ==BYD’s public ATTO 2 technical page says its mass-produced 8-in-1 electric powertrain integrates the VCU, BMS, MCU, PDU, DC-DC controller, onboard charger, drive motor, and transmission. BYD Electronics also explicitly lists infotainment, heat management, security systems, driver assistance systems, and automotive wiring among its in-house automotive electronics capabilities. ==

What BYD does **not** publicly document is a clean block diagram showing exactly how cockpit HPCs, ADAS HPCs, propulsion controllers, zonal gateways, and edge nodes are interconnected over PCIe, 100/1000BASE-T1, 10G Ethernet, CAN-FD, or LIN. Because of that, the most responsible conclusion is this: **BYD has centralized compute layers and expanding cross-domain control, but the public record does not yet justify a definitive “this is their zonal network tree” claim.** The likely architecture is Ethernet-rich in the upper layers and CAN-FD/LIN-rich toward actuators and body endpoints, because that is how these compute platforms are typically deployed, but BYD has not published that topology itself. 

That also answers the controller question. Consolidated central processors will **not** fully replace localized MCUs in BYD vehicles. They will replace a large portion of domain-level coordination, sensor fusion, HMI logic, update orchestration, and feature policy. They will not eliminate the need for low-cost, low-power, safety-local edge nodes for actuation, wake-up, fallback control, lighting, doors, seats, thermal valves, battery supervision, and local diagnostics. AUTOSAR itself still distinguishes between deeply embedded systems and higher-level compute platforms for exactly this reason, and BYD’s own 8-in-1 powertrain integration still names multiple control functions that remain physically close to power electronics and actuators. 

My estimate, based on public disclosures and current BYD product behavior, is that in mainstream Ocean and Dynasty vehicles **roughly 35–50% of software by feature value** now lives on central or domain-central compute, while in Denza and Yangwang this rises to **roughly 55–70%**. By controller count the picture is the opposite: the majority of physical control nodes are still local MCUs or smart peripherals. This is an inference, not a published BYD figure, but it is the best fit with BYD’s disclosed powertrain integration, God’s Eye tiering, and continuing use of localized control technologies such as e4, DiSus, and DMO.
## Software stack and standards posture

The cockpit stack is the clearest part of BYD’s software story. BYD’s own DiLink site states that the platform is independently developed, based on mobile internet, AI, voice, connected-car, and big-data technologies, and is built around five layers: Di Platform, Di UI, Di Ecosystem, Di Cloud, and Di Open. The same official page says DiLink uses an **Android open system**, supports standardized APIs and open vehicle-data permissions, and is built to connect “human-machine-vehicle-cloud.” In other words, BYD’s cockpit and vehicle-cloud UX layer is not supplier-white-label software. It is one of the company’s core in-house assets. 

This also makes BYD’s software stack different from Tesla’s. Tesla’s architecture is more monolithic and vertically software-owning end to end, whereas BYD’s is more accurately described as **in-house application and integration software over a mixed partner substrate**. The user-facing experience, app ecosystem decisions, cloud control, and vehicle integration logic are BYD-led. ==The compute silicon, some ADAS algorithms, and likely parts of the low-level middleware remain heterogeneous across suppliers and vehicle tiers.==

The operating-system picture is therefore uneven. Public BYD material directly confirms Android in the DiLink cockpit. It does **not** publish a vehicle-wide OS bill of materials that cleanly says “cockpit = Android Automotive,” “HPC base = Linux,” or “safety partition = QNX.” QNX, however, remains one of the most credible candidates for mixed-criticality consolidation in any BYD high-performance controller because QNX Hypervisor is explicitly designed to co-run QNX, Android, and Linux on a single SoC, and QNX OS for Safety and the QNX ADAS foundation are certified to ISO 26262 ASIL D. So the technically sound reading is: **Android is publicly visible; QNX-style safety partitioning is highly plausible; Linux almost certainly exists somewhere in the non-safety service and development chain; but BYD has not publicly documented the exact domain-by-domain deployment.** 

The same caution applies to hypervisors and vECUs. BYD has not published a company-wide vECU program comparable to what European SDV programs or SOAFEE advocates describe. Nor have I found public evidence that BYD is marketing a cloud-native vehicle software toolchain under its own name. What I *did* find is that BYD’s public software posture emphasizes open APIs, vehicle-cloud services, and rapid feature rollout rather than publicly advertising a standards-centered developer stack. That suggests BYD’s virtualization and vECU work is probably real internally, but it is not an identity pillar in the way it is for some Western SDV programs. 

On middleware and service orientation, BYD looks pragmatic rather than doctrinaire. DiLink clearly exposes vehicle data and APIs and is organized as a platform, which is compatible with SOA ideas. But BYD does not present itself as a pure “everything in-house, all proprietary protocols” company. Its reality is closer to **BYD-owned orchestration plus standardized supplier layers wherever useful**. That is also consistent with its external partnerships in ADAS and AI.

BYD’s public posture toward software consortia is notably understated. AUTOSAR’s public partner directories show a large global ecosystem, but I found no visible BYD listing in the public partnership pages I checked, and the SOAFEE member pages I checked likewise showed no public BYD presence. By contrast, BYD did join the IATF in 2026. That combination suggests a company focused more on **industrial quality-system legitimacy and fast product execution** than on visible authorship of global SDV software standards. It almost certainly uses industry standards where commercially necessary, but it is not publicly leading AUTOSAR or SOAFEE as of June 2026. 

For functional safety, BYD’s most credible path is the standard mixed-criticality pattern used across the industry: keep hard real-time and high-ASIL functions in isolated safety partitions or dedicated controllers, keep infotainment and rich apps in non-safety domains, and use safety-certified RTOS and hypervisor layers where consolidation is unavoidable. QNX’s public materials explicitly describe this architecture, including Android/Linux/QNX co-residency and ASIL-D-certified safety foundations for ADAS. Because BYD now sells integrated cockpit/ADAS experiences and is introducing its own ASIL-oriented driving silicon, this is the design logic that best fits the available evidence, even though BYD has not publicly released the actual safety case package. 

## ADAS, AI, and silicon strategy

The cleanest way to read BYD’s ADAS strategy is as a **controlled migration from partner-assisted intelligence to progressively in-house intelligence**. The 2021 Reuters report on the Momenta joint venture showed BYD was willing to co-develop L2+ capabilities rather than build everything alone from day one. Reuters also later reported Huawei’s Qiankun ADAS being used on a Fangchengbao Bao 5 smart-driving variant. At the same time, BYD has steadily expanded its self-branded God’s Eye stack and, by 2026, was confident enough to pair that stack with its self-developed XUANJI A3 driving SoC and even offer full-damage coverage for Urban NOA in China. 

In practice, this means BYD’s ADAS stack is **not fully outsourced and not fully self-contained**. The mass-market deployment layer is now visibly under BYD’s own product banner, but the ecosystem beneath it remains mixed. Horizon Robotics is a strategic low-cost ADAS silicon partner for DiPilot 100. NVIDIA has been a central compute partner for next-generation Dynasty and Ocean models since at least 2023 and remains part of BYD’s future L4 direction via DRIVE Hyperion in 2026. Huawei remains a premium external option on selected products. Momenta has been both a JV partner and, by multiple market accounts, part of the algorithmic route for some premium programs. 

The tiering of the God’s Eye family clarifies the silicon landscape. God’s Eye C is the democratization tier: horizon-priced, camera-heavy, radar-assisted, and intended for large-volume BYD-branded vehicles. Horizon’s official release confirms that DiPilot 100 launched with Journey 6 silicon to make advanced smart driving affordable at scale. God’s Eye B is the middle and upper tier, now even optional across the lineup in LiDAR form, and is the place where BYD is visibly trying to compress premium capability into lower price bands. God’s Eye A remains the flagship tier for Yangwang-class products.

The strategic inflection point is XUANJI A3. Once BYD publicly unveiled its own 4 nm driving SoC, the company moved from “OEM that integrates other people’s chips very well” to “OEM that intends to own at least the mainstream economics of AD compute.” That matters financially and organizationally. If BYD can internalize enough of the drive-compute BOM while keeping Horizon, NVIDIA, and Huawei as optional or high-end complements, it can do to ADAS silicon what it already did to blades, power electronics, and drive units: turn a premium feature into a scale-manufactured house competency.

Large-model and AI ecosystem partnerships show the same hybrid pattern. BYD’s own XUANJI AI Large Model is positioned as a multimodal vehicular AI spanning more than 300 scenarios. But BYD is simultaneously partnering outward. Alibaba Cloud’s developer community documented BYD cockpit integrations around Tongyi, including AI wallpaper, emotional-companion functions, and a Mobile-Agent implementation that can visually understand the screen and operate apps in the cockpit. MarkLines, citing Volcano Engine’s official announcement, reported that ByteDance’s Doubao model was deeply integrated into DiLink across Yangwang, Denza, Fangchengbao, Dynasty, and Ocean brands. This is a strong sign that BYD is not betting on one universal foundation model. It is building a **multi-vendor AI application layer** over its own vehicle platform. 

For end-to-end AI and next-wave autonomy, BYD’s strongest public partner today is NVIDIA rather than an open-source consortium. NVIDIA’s 2026 Hyperion announcement explicitly placed BYD in a level-4-ready development path and tied that path to standardized compute, sensors, networking, safety systems, reasoning models, and simulation tools. This is important because it implies BYD’s premium and future-AV work will still rely on external high-end ecosystems for some time, even as BYD internalizes more production silicon in L2+/L3 domains. 
## Connectivity, OTA, and the software-defined organization

BYD’s official connected-car architecture starts with DiLink, not with a separate “telematics strategy” slide. The company’s own platform page says DiLink is designed to connect human, machine, vehicle, and cloud; it exposes standardized APIs and vehicle data permissions; and its Di Cloud layer supports remote control, vehicle-status monitoring, location-related data, service booking, and cloud-assisted help through the BYD app. Regional DiLink support pages also confirm Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and LTE connectivity and describe cloud service as part of normal vehicle management. That is enough to conclude that BYD’s connectivity model is **ground-up vehicle-cloud integration**, not a later dealer add-on.

The OTA system is similarly real, but BYD is less transparent about internals than Tesla. Public BYD pages in Japan list model-specific OTA versions and change logs. As of June 2026, the official Japanese pages showed ATTO 3 public OTA versions in May 2024, September 2024, September 2025, and June 2026, while SEALION 7 showed public OTAs in August 2025 and April 2026. The update notes include UI renovations, new apps, ADAS tuning, radar-detection stability improvements, navigation upgrades, and user-account functions. In other words, BYD is not only updating infotainment skins; it is shipping changes that touch ADAS behavior, navigation, app ecosystems, and vehicle settings. 

What BYD does **not** publicly disclose in comparable detail is the underlying fail-safe mechanism: dual-bank flashing, A/B slots, delta compression, secure boot chains, or per-module rollback logic. Chinese tech press reported that BYD launched an official OTA information lookup portal in 2026 and that rollouts are phased based on the vehicle’s actual software state. That strongly implies version-aware orchestration across mixed module baselines. But the public evidence stops short of revealing the full backend design. So the responsible engineering judgment is: **BYD clearly has a mature orchestrated OTA program, but its package architecture and rollback mechanics are not publicly documented to the same degree as its feature-level release notes.**

Measured only from public release notes in export markets, BYD’s OTA cadence appears **moderate rather than hyper-aggressive**: roughly one to three visible major releases per model per year, depending on region and product age. That is materially better than traditional OEM practice, but it is still less continuous and less transparent than Tesla’s long-standing OTA discipline, which includes feature updates and even regulatory recall remedies delivered over the air. 

The organizational structure behind this is getting more software-native. In early 2024, Chinese auto media reported that BYD reorganized its smart cockpit and smart driving teams into a broader intelligent technology institute, explicitly to improve linkage between cockpit and ADAS development. Around the same period, Wang Chuanfu said BYD had more than 4,000 ADAS engineers, with more than 3,000 software engineers, and announced more than RMB 100 billion of intelligent-technology investment over three years. That is exactly what you would expect from a company shifting from component leadership to software-led system leadership: more platform architecture, more shared products, and fewer isolated digital teams attached to individual car lines. 

BYD’s cost and velocity edge follows directly from this organization plus its manufacturing structure. The company already owns major pieces of the hardware stack: batteries through FinDreams Battery, automotive electronics through BYD Electronics, semiconductors through BYD Semiconductor, and vehicle-platform integration through its own product and engineering organizations. Its public materials now also stress comprehensive ownership of key EV systems such as the mass-produced 8-in-1 powertrain, while Reuters has repeatedly noted that BYD’s vertical integration underpins margins and cost control even in price-war conditions. That means a software change at BYD can propagate across a stack whose interfaces are increasingly internal, which reduces coordination cost and shortens the path from feature concept to bill-of-materials decision to production.

### SDV litmus test

If the litmus test for an SDV is “does the car materially improve after SOP through software, and is the architecture designed to make that normal rather than exceptional,” then BYD **passes**, but not as cleanly as Tesla. BYD has in-house cockpit software, connected cloud services, OTA rollouts that change UI, apps, navigation, and ADAS tuning, a cross-domain intelligence architecture, and a growing internal ADAS and silicon stack. Tesla still leads on stack homogeneity, OTA maturity, and the cleanliness of the software-first product narrative; Tesla’s own support pages show routine OTA delivery and even recall fixes over the air. BYD, by contrast, still shows more regional fragmentation, more supplier heterogeneity, and less public transparency around software internals.

Against Xiaomi, the comparison is subtler. Xiaomi is culturally more software-native and already publishes frontier AD research, including a 2026 world-model paper for autonomous driving. But Xiaomi still lacks BYD’s industrial reach, multi-brand deployment depth, and vertically integrated hardware manufacturing base at automotive scale. So Xiaomi may look more “AI-first,” while BYD today looks more “industrialized SDV at massive scale.” 

So my definitive assessment is this: **Tesla remains the cleanest benchmark for a true software-defined production vehicle company; Xiaomi is the sharper AI-native challenger; BYD is the strongest example of an SDV architecture being industrialized for mass-market price bands.** That is why BYD is so strategically dangerous. It does not need to be the most elegant software company if it can be the fastest company at turning software architecture into millions of saleable cars.

### Differentiation playbook

BYD’s architecture creates four competitive advantages that are difficult for rivals to match simultaneously.

The first is **cost compression through vertical integration**. BYD can co-optimize battery, power electronics, semiconductors, infotainment hardware, thermal systems, and vehicle integration because a striking amount of that stack is inside the group. This reduces interface tax, speeds sourcing decisions, and makes feature-downward migration economically viable.

The second is **tiered reuse rather than one-size-fits-all purity**. BYD does not insist that a Seagull and a Yangwang use the same software and compute philosophy at every level. Instead it uses God’s Eye A/B/C tiers, e3/e4/DMO/Super e-Platform variants, and a mixed silicon portfolio. Purists may dislike that heterogeneity, but it is exactly how BYD turns advanced features into viable BOMs across a huge price ladder. 

The third is **software where the customer notices it**. DiLink, Di Cloud, OTA changes, AI cockpit agents, and app ecosystems all sit in the customer-visible zone. That makes BYD feel more software-rich than many traditional OEMs even when its underlying stack is not fully homogeneous. 

The fourth is **industrial scale as a software weapon**. Many SDV strategies are technically sound but commercially trapped in low-volume segments. BYD is doing the opposite: it uses millions of annual units, large engineering teams, and very high R&D spending to force software and ADAS down into price points that competitors thought would stay hardware-constrained for years. That is why BYD’s architecture matters globally. Its real innovation is not just centralized compute; it is the ability to make centralized-compute-era vehicles affordable, fast to launch, and increasingly global. 

In short, BYD’s playbook is not “be Tesla.” It is “take enough of the SDV stack in-house, partner where it accelerates us, and industrialize the result faster than anyone else.” From an architectural point of view, that makes BYD less pure than Tesla. From a market point of view, it may make BYD more dangerous.
